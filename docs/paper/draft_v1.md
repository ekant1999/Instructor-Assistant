# Draft v1: Routed PDF Extraction + VLM Backend Study

## Title

Routing PDF Extraction Across Native Parsing and Vision-Language OCR for Heterogeneous Documents

## Abstract

PDF-to-Markdown extraction remains brittle across heterogeneous document regimes, including born-digital research papers, hybrid reports with screenshots and code, and text-heavy report-style documents. Single-path systems typically overfit one regime: native layout parsers are strong on structured scholarly PDFs, while OCR- or VLM-centered systems can be more robust on visually complex or text-heavy documents. We study this problem through a manually curated, source-validated benchmark of 120 PDFs spanning research, hybrid, and text-heavy document types. We compare three end-to-end systems: `scholar_parser`, `ocr_agent`, and a routed hybrid system, `improved_ocr_agent`, which preserves the original OCR path for OCR-routed pages while dispatching non-OCR scholarly pages to `scholar_parser` and non-OCR text-heavy pages to native `ocr_agent`. On the expanded internal benchmark, the routed system achieves the strongest overall structure metrics, with mean heading F1 `0.509` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.523`, compared with `0.416/0.309` for `scholar_parser` and `0.422/0.450` for `ocr_agent`, while preserving figure recall `0.980`. The family-level breakdown is sharper than in the original pilot: `scholar_parser` remains strongest on research papers, `ocr_agent` remains strongest on text-heavy reports, and the expanded hybrid set remains difficult for all three systems. We then extend the study with `olmOCR-Bench` to compare specialized OCR VLMs against general-purpose multimodal VLMs, and evaluate whether structured prompting plus document-aware post-processing can improve the OCR quality of general-purpose models. Together, these results support a simple conclusion: extraction quality depends strongly on document regime, routing improves end-to-end performance, and OCR backend quality is a key factor in how much a routed system can improve.

## 1. Introduction

Extracting faithful Markdown from PDFs is still an unstable problem. The difficulty is not only OCR quality, but also the heterogeneity of modern documents. Born-digital research papers, hybrid reports with screenshots and embedded code, and text-heavy report-style documents place different demands on an extraction system. A parser that is strong on scholarly headings, captions, and appendices may still fail on report-style section labels or OCR-heavy pages. Conversely, an OCR- or VLM-centered system may preserve raw page text while producing weak section structure or noisy captions. In practice, this means that single-path extraction systems often perform well only on a subset of document regimes.

Our starting point was a direct comparison between two existing systems. The first, hereafter `scholar_parser`, is stronger on structured scholarly parsing, including sections, figures, tables, and quality-controlled Markdown export. The second, `ocr_agent`, provides page-level routing with a VLM-based OCR path and is naturally stronger on some text-heavy and OCR-heavy cases. These differences suggested that the problem should not be framed as choosing one system over the other. The more useful framing is that different pages and different documents benefit from different extraction backends.

Based on that observation, we build a routed extraction system, `improved_ocr_agent`, that preserves the original OCR path but routes non-OCR pages by document regime. Scholarly and structured-visual non-OCR pages are handled by `scholar_parser`, while text-heavy non-OCR pages remain on the native `ocr_agent` path. This allows the system to retain the strengths of both pipelines without rewriting the OCR path itself. The result is a more robust end-to-end system on heterogeneous PDFs.

However, routing alone is not the full research story. OCR backend quality also matters, especially for OCR-heavy pages that cannot be handled well by native parsing. A paper based only on heuristic routing and engineering comparisons would be weak. For that reason, we extend the study in two directions. First, we formalize our internal benchmark as a manually curated dataset with gold annotations and document-type stratification. Second, we add an external OCR backend evaluation using `olmOCR-Bench`, comparing a specialized OCR/document VLM against general-purpose multimodal VLMs. This lets us ask not only whether routing helps, but also how OCR backend choice affects routed system quality, and whether structured prompting plus document-aware post-processing can make general-purpose VLMs more competitive OCR backends.

### Research Questions

The paper is organized around three research questions:

- `RQ1`: How does a specialized OCR/document VLM compare with a general-purpose multimodal VLM on OCR-heavy benchmark tasks?
- `RQ2`: Does category-aware routing across extraction backends improve end-to-end PDF-to-Markdown quality over single-path baselines on heterogeneous documents?
- `RQ3`: How far can inference-time improvements such as structured prompting and document-aware post-processing make a general-purpose VLM a more viable OCR backend inside a routed extraction system?

This paper makes four contributions. First, we present a manually curated PDF-to-Markdown benchmark spanning 120 documents across research papers, hybrid documents, and text-heavy reports, with source-validated gold annotations for structure and assets. Second, we compare three end-to-end systems and show that category-aware routing improves end-to-end extraction quality on heterogeneous PDFs, even though the expanded hybrid regime remains difficult. Third, we add a backend-level OCR study on `olmOCR-Bench`, comparing specialized OCR VLMs against general-purpose multimodal VLMs. Fourth, we define an inference-time improvement recipe for general-purpose VLM OCR based on structured prompting and document-aware post-processing.

## 2. Benchmark Dataset and Metrics

Our internal benchmark now contains 120 manually curated PDFs divided evenly across three document families: 40 `ResearchPapers`, 40 `Hybrid` documents, and 40 `TextHeavy` documents. The research subset contains born-digital academic papers with equations, figures, tables, appendices, and multi-column layouts. The hybrid subset includes non-academic assignments, lab reports, and mixed-layout documents with screenshots, code, or embedded visual content. The text-heavy subset contains report-style documents whose structure depends more on prose headings such as `Section N:` than on scholarly front matter or formal captions.

Each document is paired with a gold annotation file created through manual curation against the source PDF. The gold schema includes validated heading lists, section anchor snippets, figure annotations, and table annotations. This design keeps the benchmark focused on document fidelity rather than raw string similarity. We do not compare Markdown files line by line; instead, we normalize extracted outputs into a common representation and compare them against curated structural ground truth.

We report heading precision, recall, and F1; heading order score; anchor recall; anchor assignment accuracy; figure recall; and table recall. Anchor assignment accuracy is especially important for this benchmark because it directly measures whether extracted text appears under the correct section heading, rather than merely appearing somewhere in the output. In addition to gold-based metrics, we track runtime and intrinsic Markdown-quality signals such as duplicate heading count and empty section count.

The expanded benchmark results still show strong regime effects, but they also reveal a harder hybrid regime than the original pilot suggested. On the overall benchmark, `scholar_parser` reaches heading F1 `0.416` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.309`, with perfect figure recall. `ocr_agent` reaches heading F1 `0.422` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.450`, but with much weaker figure recall. The routed `improved_ocr_agent` achieves the strongest overall structure metrics, with heading F1 `0.509` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.523` while preserving figure recall `0.980`. By document family, `scholar_parser` remains strongest on research papers, `ocr_agent` is strongest on text-heavy reports, and the routed system preserves both advantages while still leaving the expanded hybrid set largely unsolved.

## 3. Systems and Backends

We compare three end-to-end systems. `scholar_parser` is the paper-facing name for the project’s native structured extraction pipeline and is particularly strong on scholarly parsing, section assignment, figure extraction, table extraction, and Markdown export quality. `ocr_agent` is a hybrid extractor with page-level routing, including a VLM-backed OCR path, and is stronger on some text-heavy and OCR-heavy cases. `improved_ocr_agent` preserves the original OCR page path but routes non-OCR scholarly or structured-visual pages through `scholar_parser` while preserving native `ocr_agent` handling for non-OCR text-heavy pages. Page outputs are then merged in original page order.

This design is important for two reasons. First, it avoids changing the OCR path itself, which keeps OCR behavior comparable to the original system. Second, it makes the routing logic interpretable: OCR-routed pages stay on OCR, scholarly non-OCR pages use the stronger structured parser, and text-heavy non-OCR pages retain the stronger native report-style handling. The result is a routed system whose gains can be attributed to dispatch decisions rather than to a wholesale rewrite of either upstream system.

At the OCR backend level, the paper treats OCR quality as a separate variable. The specialized OCR/document baseline is `olmOCR-2`, which is already integrated into the current OCR flow. The current general-purpose comparison set centers on a Qwen-family vision-language model evaluated under structured prompting and document-aware post-processing; adding a second model family such as Gemma remains future work. This backend study is not separate from the routing story; it explains why routing helps and which OCR backends are strong enough to justify inclusion in a routed pipeline.

## 4. Experimental Setup

### 4.1 Internal benchmark setup

The internal benchmark evaluates three end-to-end systems on the expanded 120-document corpus: `scholar_parser`, `ocr_agent`, and `improved_ocr_agent`. Each system is run on the full document set, and the resulting Markdown outputs are normalized and compared against manually curated gold annotations for headings, section anchors, figures, and tables. The routed system uses a fixed dispatch policy rather than per-document manual tuning: OCR-routed pages remain on the native OCR path, non-OCR scholarly or structured-visual pages are sent to `scholar_parser`, and non-OCR text-heavy pages remain on the native `ocr_agent` path. This setup makes the internal comparison a test of routing policy and backend choice rather than of ad hoc per-file rescue logic.

For the expanded 120-document rerun reported in this draft, the OCR-backed systems were executed against the same stable managed Qwen endpoint used in the backend study. Earlier internal runs used the native `olmOCR-2` service, but that endpoint was not available for the full expanded rerun. The internal results reported here should therefore be interpreted as a routed-systems comparison under a common managed OCR backend, not as a direct replication of the earlier `olmOCR-2`-backed pilot numbers.

We report heading precision, recall, and F1; heading order; anchor recall; anchor assignment accuracy; figure recall; and table recall. Anchor assignment accuracy is the main structure-sensitive metric because it measures whether extracted content is attached to the correct section rather than merely appearing somewhere in the output. Runtime and intrinsic Markdown-quality diagnostics, such as duplicate headings and empty sections, are retained as supporting signals, but the main paper conclusions are based on the gold-aligned structural metrics.

### 4.2 `olmOCR-Bench` setup

For backend-only OCR evaluation, we use `olmOCR-Bench`, which contains OCR-focused single-page test cases spanning math, tables, multi-column pages, long tiny text, headers and footers, and historical scans. Candidate generation was run with one repeat per page and benchmarked with the official `olmocr.bench.benchmark` tooling. We report the benchmark's official average score, which is computed as the average of per-JSONL benchmark-slice scores, together with the bootstrap confidence interval emitted by the benchmark tool. We also report category-level scores and slice-level breakdowns where they materially clarify model behavior.

The specialized OCR baseline is `allenai/olmOCR-2-7B-1025-FP8`, served through an OpenAI-compatible `vLLM` endpoint. The general-purpose baseline is a Qwen-family vision-language model evaluated through a stable managed OpenAI-compatible endpoint. This distinction is important operationally: earlier self-hosted Colab attempts were sufficient for pilot experiments but not stable enough for a full official benchmark run, so the formal Qwen results reported in this paper come from the managed endpoint run rather than from the unstable self-hosted setup.

### 4.3 General-purpose VLM conditions

The Qwen-family backend is evaluated under two formal benchmark conditions and one pilot-only condition. The qualitative pilot includes `Naive`, `StructuredPrompt`, and `StructuredPrompt+Post`, because that comparison was useful for quickly validating whether prompt structure mattered at all on full-document examples. The formal `olmOCR-Bench` comparison retains only the two stronger inference-time conditions: `StructuredPrompt` and `StructuredPrompt+Post`.

`StructuredPrompt` uses a document-oriented Markdown transcription prompt aligned with the project OCR pipeline. The prompt requires faithful reading order, heading preservation, conservative math rendering, and structured handling of figures and tables. `StructuredPrompt+Post` uses the same model output but adds deterministic post-processing, including repeated header and footer cleanup, heading normalization, duplicate heading removal, empty-section suppression, and page-order-preserving cleanup. This design keeps the `RQ3` claim focused on inference-time improvements rather than on model retraining.

### 4.4 Reproducibility notes and caveats

Two practical caveats matter for the reported backend study. First, the final `olmocr2` candidate set still contained `16/1403` empty pages after retry, concentrated in difficult categories such as `long_tiny_text`, `tables`, and `headers_footers`. The reported `olmOCR-2` score is therefore slightly conservative. Second, the Qwen-family benchmark results should be interpreted as the strongest stable managed-endpoint run achieved within the deadline, not as a claim about every possible self-hosted deployment of the same model family. Both caveats affect absolute numbers slightly, but neither changes the direction of the comparison.

## 5. Internal Benchmark Results

The expanded internal benchmark supports a narrower but still clear end-to-end conclusion. A single-path structured parser is not sufficient across all document regimes, and neither is a single-path OCR-centered system. `scholar_parser` is strongest on scholarly structure and figure handling, while `ocr_agent` is strongest on text-heavy report-like documents. The routed `improved_ocr_agent` produces the best overall balance across the corpus, but the results also show that the hybrid regime remains unresolved.

Overall, `scholar_parser` achieves heading F1 `0.416`, anchor assignment accuracy `0.309`, figure recall `1.000`, and table recall `0.957`. `ocr_agent` achieves heading F1 `0.422`, anchor assignment accuracy `0.450`, figure recall `0.573`, and table recall `1.000`. `improved_ocr_agent` improves the overall structure metrics to heading F1 `0.509` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.523`, while maintaining figure recall `0.980` and table recall `0.952`. These results indicate that routing still improves document structure on the expanded corpus, but the margin is now smaller and more dependent on document family than it appeared in the original 25-document pilot.

The per-type breakdown clarifies where the gains come from. On `ResearchPapers`, `scholar_parser` remains slightly stronger than the routed system on both heading F1 (`0.684` vs. `0.629`) and anchor assignment (`0.572` vs. `0.560`), but the routed system stays close and remains substantially ahead of `ocr_agent`. On `TextHeavy` documents, the routed system exactly matches native `ocr_agent` (`0.748` heading F1, `0.856` anchor assignment), which is precisely the intended behavior of the revised non-OCR router. The main new finding is the hybrid regime: on the expanded `Hybrid` set, all three systems remain weak, with heading F1 staying near `0.15` and anchor assignment topping out at roughly `0.152`. These results justify the claim that backend strengths differ by document regime, but they also show that routing alone does not solve weakly structured hybrid documents.

## 6. External OCR Backend Study

The remaining question is how OCR backend choice affects extraction quality on OCR-heavy content. To answer this, we use `olmOCR-Bench` as a standardized OCR evaluation and compare a specialized OCR VLM against a Qwen-family general-purpose multimodal VLM. The specialized baseline is `allenai/olmOCR-2-7B-1025-FP8`, which is already the native OCR backend in the current OCR pipeline. The Qwen-family backend was evaluated under two official benchmark conditions: `StructuredPrompt` and `StructuredPrompt+Post`. We retain the naive prompt as a qualitative pilot baseline, but the formal benchmark comparison is centered on the two strongest inference-time Qwen conditions.

### 6.1 Preliminary Pilot: `olmOCR-2` vs `Qwen/Qwen3-VL-8B-Instruct`

Before running the full `olmOCR-Bench` comparison, we ran a controlled three-document pilot on one research paper, one hybrid assignment, and one text-heavy report. In this pilot, `olmOCR-2` was run on the full PDF directly, while Qwen was run page-by-page across the full PDF and then merged into one Markdown document per file. Qwen was evaluated under three conditions: `Naive`, `StructuredPrompt`, and `StructuredPrompt+Post`. The pilot is qualitative rather than benchmark-level, but it is already informative for `RQ3`.

The results show a consistent pattern. On the research paper, naive Qwen is already serviceable, but structured prompting substantially improves section hierarchy and overall Markdown organization; the post-processed output is the cleanest Qwen variant, although `olmOCR-2` still preserves structured assets such as tables more faithfully. On the hybrid assignment, naive Qwen is not usable as a final output, while structured prompting plus post-processing makes the output much more coherent, preserving page structure, screenshots, and some tables; however, `olmOCR-2` still appears stronger overall on mixed visual content. On the text-heavy report, structured prompting alone does not fully solve footer leakage, but the post-processing stage removes those page-footer artifacts and produces a much cleaner final document. Taken together, these pilot results support the intended `RQ3` claim: structured prompting is necessary for general-purpose VLM OCR on difficult document pages, and document-aware post-processing can materially improve output quality, especially on text-heavy documents.

### 6.2 `olmOCR-Bench` Results

The formal `olmOCR-Bench` run confirms the qualitative pilot. `olmOCR-2` substantially outperforms the Qwen-family baseline across every major metric group. The specialized model reaches an average benchmark score of `74.7% ± 1.0%`, compared with `50.0% ± 1.2%` for `StructuredPrompt` Qwen and `52.3% ± 1.2%` for `StructuredPrompt+Post` Qwen. Thus, document-aware post-processing yields a measurable improvement over structured prompting alone (`+2.3` points), but the best Qwen condition still remains `22.4` points behind the specialized OCR/document model.

The category breakdown clarifies where the gap comes from. `olmOCR-2` is stronger on every reported metric group: `absent` (`95.3%` vs. `66.3%`), `math` (`72.5%` vs. `45.1%`), `order` (`68.5%` vs. `38.4%`), `present` (`51.5%` vs. `28.4%`), and `table` (`76.7%` vs. `44.3%`). The same pattern holds at the benchmark-slice level. On `headers_footers.jsonl`, `olmOCR-2` reaches `95.4%` compared with `64.7%` for Qwen post-processing. On `long_tiny_text.jsonl`, the specialized model reaches `58.4%` compared with `29.4%`. On `multi_column.jsonl`, the gap is `76.1%` versus `42.6%`. On `table_tests.jsonl`, the gap is `76.7%` versus `44.4%`. Even on the more difficult historical material, `olmOCR-2` remains ahead on `old_scans.jsonl` (`44.3%` vs. `31.6%`) and `old_scans_math.jsonl` (`76.0%` vs. `66.2%`).

These results support two conclusions. First, `RQ1` is answered clearly: a specialized OCR/document VLM remains substantially stronger than the current Qwen-family general-purpose baseline on OCR-heavy benchmark tasks. Second, `RQ3` is still supported, but more narrowly than the pilot alone might suggest: structured prompting plus document-aware post-processing improves a general-purpose VLM, especially on difficult text-presence recovery, yet it does not close the gap on math, reading order, or table fidelity. In other words, inference-time improvements help, but they do not remove the advantage of a specialized document OCR backend.

Even with that caveat, however, the margin over the Qwen-family alternatives is large enough that the main conclusion is unchanged.

### 6.3 Toward Self-Verifying Region OCR

The benchmark results also clarify why the current Qwen-family pipeline remains limited. Structured prompting plus post-processing improves difficult text-presence recovery, but it does not materially close the gap on `math`, `order`, `table`, `multi_column`, or `long_tiny_text`. This suggests that the main limitation of a general-purpose VLM OCR backend is not only raw text recognition. The deeper limitation is the lack of document-specific inference structure. A single page-level transcription call, even with a stronger prompt, is still being asked to solve layout analysis, reading-order recovery, region typing, transcription, and structural cleanup all at once.

To address that limitation, the next stage of `RQ3` is a proposed inference-time method we call `SVR-OCR` (`Self-Verifying Region OCR`). The method replaces single-pass full-page OCR with a structured pipeline that first predicts a page layout graph, then selectively refines only difficult or structurally important regions, and finally verifies local outputs before document assembly. In the first pass, the model or layout subsystem predicts typed blocks, block bounding boxes, reading-order edges, and rough confidence estimates. In the second pass, only low-confidence or high-value regions such as dense tiny-text blocks, tables, equations, and ambiguous multi-column areas are reprocessed with block-specific prompts and higher-resolution crops. This converts the general-purpose VLM from a generic page transcriber into a typed document processor.

The key idea is local verification. For each refined block, `SVR-OCR` renders the candidate output back into a synthetic representation and compares it against the original crop. Text blocks can be checked through lightweight rendered-text similarity, table blocks through rendered HTML table structure, and equation blocks through LaTeX rendering. Candidates that verify well are accepted; candidates that fail are reranked or repaired with a local correction prompt that has access to the crop, the previous output, and the verifier failure reason. The final page is then assembled from verified blocks and explicit reading-order edges, followed by lightweight document-level reconciliation such as recurring header/footer suppression and heading normalization.

This design matters for two reasons. First, it is still an inference-time method rather than a new trained OCR model, so it remains compatible with the paper's emphasis on practical backend improvement without fine-tuning. Second, it spends extra compute only where the current Qwen-family path is weakest, rather than repeatedly rerunning whole pages. In that sense, `SVR-OCR` is intended as the natural successor to `StructuredPrompt+Post`: instead of adding more global prompt instructions, it introduces explicit structure, selective high-resolution refinement, and verifier-guided local repair. If successful, this should improve exactly the categories where the current benchmark gap is largest, especially `long_tiny_text`, `multi_column`, `table_tests`, and math-heavy slices.

In the current system, the first deployment boundary for `SVR-OCR` is the OCR branch only. OCR-routed pages in `improved_ocr_agent` would use `SVR-OCR` in place of the current single-pass general-purpose VLM OCR call, while non-OCR scholarly pages would continue to use `scholar_parser` and non-OCR text-heavy pages would remain on the native `ocr_agent` path. This preserves the main routed-system result while making `RQ3` more ambitious: the question is no longer only whether a better prompt helps a general-purpose VLM, but whether document-specific inference structure can make a general-purpose backend substantially more viable inside a routed PDF extraction system.

## 7. Discussion: Answers to `RQ1`-`RQ3`

### 7.1 `RQ1`: OCR backends differ substantially on OCR-heavy tasks

`RQ1` asked how specialized OCR/document VLMs compare with general-purpose multimodal VLMs on OCR-heavy benchmark tasks. The answer is clear in the current backend study: the specialized OCR model is substantially stronger. On `olmOCR-Bench`, `olmOCR-2` reaches `74.7% ± 1.0%`, while the strongest Qwen-family condition, `qwen_structured_post`, reaches `52.3% ± 1.2%`. The gap is not confined to one benchmark slice. It persists across `math`, `order`, `table`, `headers_footers`, `long_tiny_text`, and `multi_column`, which are exactly the categories that stress document structure rather than only raw token recovery.

This matters for system design. If OCR-heavy pages are a meaningful part of the workload, backend choice is not a minor implementation detail. It materially changes the ceiling of the OCR branch. In the current evidence, a specialized OCR/document backend remains the most defensible choice when high-fidelity document extraction is required.

### 7.2 `RQ2`: category-aware routing improves end-to-end extraction on heterogeneous PDFs

`RQ2` asked whether routing across heterogeneous extraction backends improves end-to-end PDF-to-Markdown quality over single-path baselines. The expanded 120-document benchmark still answers this positively, but more cautiously than the original 25-document pilot. `improved_ocr_agent` achieves the strongest overall structure metrics, with heading F1 `0.509` and anchor assignment accuracy `0.523`, compared with `0.416` and `0.309` for `scholar_parser`, and `0.422` and `0.450` for `ocr_agent`.

The more important point is why the routed system wins. It does not dominate because one upstream system is universally better. It wins because the benchmark is heterogeneous and the systems are complementary. `scholar_parser` is strongest on scholarly structure, `ocr_agent` is strongest on text-heavy report-style material, and the routed system preserves both advantages by dispatching non-OCR pages differently while leaving OCR-routed pages on the OCR path. At the same time, the expanded hybrid set shows the limit of routing alone: when the documents themselves are weakly structured and visually irregular, none of the three systems is strong. This is the central systems result of the paper: for mixed PDF collections, choosing one extractor is the wrong framing, but routing needs strong backends and still does not eliminate every regime-specific failure.

### 7.3 `RQ3`: inference-time improvements help general-purpose VLM OCR, but do not close the gap

`RQ3` asked whether structured prompting and document-aware post-processing can make general-purpose VLMs competitive enough to serve as effective OCR backends inside a routed pipeline. The current answer is qualified. The Qwen-family results show that inference-time improvements do help: `StructuredPrompt+Post` improves over `StructuredPrompt` by `+2.3` benchmark points overall, and the gain is especially visible on difficult text-presence recovery, including `long_tiny_text`. The qualitative pilot also shows that structured prompting is necessary for hybrid and text-heavy pages, and that post-processing can remove some footer leakage and document-cleanup artifacts.

However, the same results also show the limit of the current recipe. Prompting plus post-processing does not materially close the gap on `math`, `order`, `table`, or `multi_column`. So the right conclusion is not that general-purpose VLMs are already interchangeable with specialized OCR/document models. The stronger conclusion is that they are promising, but their remaining failure modes are structural. That is why the next step in `RQ3` should not be another global prompt variant. It should be a more explicit inference architecture such as `SVR-OCR`, which attacks the missing pieces directly: region typing, selective high-resolution refinement, verification, and local repair.

### 7.4 Bridge across the three research questions

Taken together, the three research questions describe one coherent argument rather than three separate experiments. `RQ2` shows that heterogeneous documents benefit from routing rather than single-path extraction. `RQ1` shows that OCR backend quality strongly affects how capable the OCR branch can be. `RQ3` then asks how far a general-purpose backend can be improved before it becomes a more serious routed-system option. The present results imply that routing is already worthwhile, specialized OCR remains the strongest backend for OCR-heavy pages, and inference-time improvements are useful but not yet sufficient to eliminate the specialized-backend advantage. This bridge is the real contribution of the paper: routed extraction quality depends jointly on document regime and backend strength, not on routing heuristics alone.

## 8. Implementation Note: Prompt and Post-Processing Contract

The formal Qwen-family benchmark conditions were implemented through the benchmark runner's `basic` and `fullv3simple` prompt templates. The `Naive` pilot condition corresponds to the simplest transcription contract: transcribe the page to Markdown, preserve visible text in reading order, and do not summarize. The stronger benchmark condition, `StructuredPrompt`, follows the `fullv3simple`-aligned project OCR contract. Operationally, the prompt requires the model to return a faithful Markdown transcription in natural reading order while preserving headings, paragraphs, lists, captions, references, and footnotes; removing clearly repetitive headers and footers; converting equations to LaTeX; using HTML for tables; avoiding hallucinated content; and emitting figure placeholders in a fixed Markdown image-reference format when image regions should be preserved.

The `StructuredPrompt+Post` condition uses the same model output but adds a deterministic cleanup pass. The post-processing recipe has seven fixed steps: parse the YAML front matter; strip obvious repeated headers and footers; normalize heading markers and numbering prefixes; remove duplicate headings; remove empty sections unsupported by body text; normalize figure and table placeholder formatting; and preserve page order without merging content across pages. This matters methodologically because the gain attributed to `StructuredPrompt+Post` is not the result of retraining or hand-editing. It is a strictly inference-time improvement applied uniformly across outputs. In the present benchmark, that recipe improved the Qwen-family overall score from `50.0% ± 1.2%` to `52.3% ± 1.2%`, with the clearest gains on difficult text-presence recovery rather than on table or math structure.

## 9. Error Analysis

The combined benchmark and internal results show that the systems fail for different reasons. On the backend benchmark, the Qwen-family post-processed condition still fails `3904` tests, compared with `1910` for `olmOCR-2`. The failure distribution is especially informative. For Qwen post-processing, the dominant failure types are `math` (`1859`), `order` (`654`), `table` (`568`), and `present` (`516`). For `olmOCR-2`, the same categories remain the hardest, but at substantially lower counts: `math` (`932`), `present` (`350`), `order` (`334`), and `table` (`238`). This supports the central interpretation of `RQ3`: prompt cleanup helps a general-purpose VLM, but its remaining weaknesses are structural rather than cosmetic.

The benchmark slices make that pattern concrete. Qwen-family failures are concentrated in exactly the categories that require document-aware structure recovery: dense tiny text, multi-column reading order, and table fidelity. Representative order failures show the model interleaving distant column fragments rather than preserving local reading sequence. Representative table failures show partial cell recovery without a stable grid. Representative math failures show long expressions or operator-rich lines being dropped or normalized into non-matching forms. By contrast, `olmOCR-2` still fails on very difficult math-heavy and degraded historical pages, but those failures are more concentrated at the extreme end of the benchmark rather than spread broadly across structural categories.

The internal benchmark shows the same regime dependence at the document level. On scholarly papers such as `p118` and `p119`, the routed system stays close to `scholar_parser`, while plain `ocr_agent` loses meaningful structure. On `p118`, `scholar_parser` and `improved_ocr_agent` both remain above `0.92` heading F1, whereas `ocr_agent` drops to `0.560` heading F1 and `0.207` anchor assignment accuracy. On `p119`, the routed system nearly preserves the `scholar_parser` result, while `ocr_agent` weakens anchor assignment from `0.889` to `0.556`. These examples show that born-digital research PDFs reward strong structured parsing and careful section assignment, not only raw text recovery.

The hybrid and text-heavy examples expose a different limitation. On structured assignments such as `a005`, `a006`, and `a007`, `scholar_parser` and `improved_ocr_agent` remain strong while plain `ocr_agent` loses both headings and anchors. On visually irregular hybrid reports such as `a008` and `a009`, however, all systems score poorly on heading F1, and even the routed system does not recover a stable hierarchy. In these cases, the problem is not only backend choice; the documents themselves are weakly heading-structured and visually dominated by mixed content. On the text-heavy report `t001`, the pattern flips: `scholar_parser` is weak, while both `ocr_agent` and `improved_ocr_agent` achieve strong heading and anchor performance. This is exactly the behavior the router was designed to exploit.

Taken together, the error analysis reinforces the paper's main claim. The hard cases are not random outliers. They are systematic manifestations of document regime. Research-heavy pages demand strong structure and asset handling; text-heavy pages reward OCR-centered extraction; and general-purpose VLM OCR remains weakest where document structure must be inferred rather than merely transcribed.

## 10. Conclusion

This paper studied PDF-to-Markdown extraction as a routed systems problem rather than as a single-model competition. Across an expanded 120-document benchmark of heterogeneous PDFs, the results show that backend strengths differ strongly by document regime and that category-aware routing improves end-to-end extraction quality over single-path baselines. The routed `improved_ocr_agent` achieves the best overall structural performance on the internal benchmark, confirming that explicit backend selection is a stronger strategy than forcing all documents through one extractor, even though the expanded hybrid regime remains difficult.

The external backend study sharpens that result. On `olmOCR-Bench`, the specialized OCR/document model `olmOCR-2` substantially outperforms the current Qwen-family general-purpose baseline, even after structured prompting and document-aware post-processing. This means that OCR backend quality is a major determinant of how strong the OCR branch of a routed system can be. At the same time, the Qwen-family results show that inference-time improvements are still worthwhile: structured prompting and deterministic cleanup measurably improve OCR behavior, especially on difficult text-presence recovery, even though they do not yet close the gap on math, reading order, or table fidelity.

The broader implication is straightforward. For heterogeneous PDFs, robust extraction requires both routing and backend awareness. Routing solves the regime-selection problem, but the quality of the routed OCR branch still depends on the OCR backend itself. The natural next step is therefore not another single global prompt, but a more document-aware inference architecture for general-purpose VLM OCR. The proposed `SVR-OCR` direction follows directly from the current evidence: if the remaining gap is largely structural, then explicit layout reasoning, selective refinement, and verifier-guided local repair are the right mechanisms to investigate next.
